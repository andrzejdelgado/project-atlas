## The trap

Most design systems start with a simple promise: consistency. Then a
team hits an edge case. The system owner writes a policy. Next quarter
another team hits a different edge case. Another policy. By year two
the system is a thicket of rules that no one fully understands.

## The reframe

A **policy** says *this is how we do it*. A **pattern** says *here's
what we've seen work, and here's why*. The first is brittle and breaks
the moment context shifts. The second teaches the team to think.

I rewrote our entire system docs around patterns:

- Every component had a "when to reach for this" section.
- Every constraint had a "why" alongside the rule.
- Every override had a documented escape hatch.

## What changed

The system stopped feeling like a permissions system. Designers
started consulting the docs *before* asking for exceptions, because
the docs answered the question they were actually trying to ask.

Tickets to the system team dropped by ~40% in the quarter after
launch. Not because we got faster — because teams stopped needing us.

## The catch

Patterns require trust. If your org doesn't trust designers to read a
"why" and decide for themselves, patterns won't survive contact with
governance. Start with the policy-heavy version and earn the runway
to convert.
