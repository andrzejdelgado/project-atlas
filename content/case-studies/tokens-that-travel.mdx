## Context

A 200-person product org with three product teams, two platforms, and
roughly four flavours of "design tokens" — each one calling itself the
source of truth. Every theme migration broke something. Every redesign
re-litigated the same naming arguments.

## The frame

Tokens fail at scale not because the values are wrong but because the
*meaning* is unstable. We rebuilt the taxonomy in three layers:

1. **Reference** — raw, immutable values (colour ramps, spacing units).
2. **System** — semantic intent (`surface.elevated`, `text.muted`).
3. **Component** — bindings owned by the components themselves.

Teams could only consume the layer above their problem. If a designer
wanted to introduce a new colour, they had to argue at the system layer
in front of the system owners — not silently in their feature branch.

## Outcome

- Theme migrations dropped from a multi-week effort to a two-day patch.
- Token count fell by about a third while coverage *increased*.
- The argument shifted from "what do we call this colour" to "what does
  this colour mean", which turned out to be a much shorter conversation.

## What I'd do differently

Insist on the three-layer model on day one, not month four. Every team
that joined late carried a different mental model and we paid for that
in onboarding cost for a year.
