### A) Saturn Heavy — multi-brand design system + Terraform programme

**Role.** Principal Product Designer and joint Head of Project alongside the Principal Software Engineer. End-to-end ownership of the design system from MVP through twelve-brand migration to handover.

**Window.** December 2024 → March 2026 (15-month frontend transformation; handover during paternity leave).

**Team scale.** Design pod began as a single designer; collapsed organisationally from 17 → 5 during restructuring; rebuilt to 15+ designers including DesignOps. Engineering scaled from 14 to 100+ across multiple internal and outsourced (3rd-party) teams. At peak the dynamic-duo managed 110+ people.

**Brands & technical scope.**
- 12 brands shipped from a single token taxonomy.
- New-brand adoption became "matter of a week" thanks to Meta Tokens + Surfaces/Backgrounds + Tones + Surface Deck Levels resolving design decisions automatically.
- Three-tier token taxonomy (Primitive / Meta / Component) reduced potential tokens from ~36,600 to ~200 at Meta level (which drove ~80% of decisions).

**Tooling stack.**
- **Figma** (Saturn Heavy proper) — procurement secured after years of advocacy.
- **Adobe XD** (MVP only — shipped under deadline because Figma landed in October).
- **Storybook** as single source of truth (codename **Halo UI**); **Jupyter** SSR build.
- **Panda CSS** for runtime theming and semantic-tier tokens.
- **W3C design tokens** exported as JSON.
- **Mattermost** as cross-team engineering channel.
- **Semantic Atlas** — internal tool built for governance/automation at scale.
- **React / Next.js**.

**Concepts he coined (used as systems language at The Workshop).**
- **Surfaces vs. Backgrounds** — a surface is the base layer a UI sits on; a background is the fill of a component on that surface. They can share a hex code but behave differently when environment changes. Lets the system make design decisions automatically across brands and modes.
- **Tones** — luminance-banded classification (light/even/dark) using **APCA** methodology (not WCAG) for contrast.
- **Token Mapping** — three-tier mapping that allows ~200 meta tokens to drive ~80% of system decisions.
- **Surface Deck Levels** — defined deck-level surface colors that automate UI placement decisions.
- **Composite Components** — pre-engineered atomic components that compose into highly complex surfaces (e.g. sports coupons with thousands of permutations).
- **Component-Level Dark Mode** — solution for legacy/Operator-mandated always-light parts of UI, where a component knows its own mode independent of brand mode.
- **Progressive Design Model (PDM)** — design-side equivalent of a CI/CD pipeline; an 8-stage workflow with procedural Peer Checks after each incremental delivery.

**Outcomes (real-user, CrUX field data, mobile).**
- **LCP −54%, FCP −71%, TTFB −79%** on migrated brand vs. legacy brand.
- Comparable desktop gains.
- 60+ components re-authored through Project **Titan**.
- Design-delivery throughput improved ~14× by mid-2025 vs. before Saturn Heavy (his internal data).
- Cross-collaboration efficiency lifted **4.5×** after introducing Agile in the design team alone.
- The system continued to ship without him from the first week of his paternity leave onward — *which was the goal from day one*. "A design system that needs its lead in the building every day is not a system, it is a person standing in for one."

**Things he says he'd do differently next time.**
1. Push harder for a Panda-CSS-compliant UI library instead of building basic components from scratch (he was in the room when the decision was made and feels he should have pushed more).
2. Push earlier for lo-fi wireframes + written specs in every ticket to free up design capacity for higher-value work.

### B) Feelsfast — perceived performance, then platform, then AI skill

**Origin (April 2024).** Inside an iGaming operator (under NDA). He led a 5-day research sprint to figure out why the product *felt* slow even on days when measured performance was acceptable. Followed by ~1 month of implementation.

**Method constraints (real).**
- No direct user access; player feedback arrived through an intermediary.
- Worked from **1,000+** recorded feedback calls + the operator's existing personas/data.
- Used **web.dev pagespeed** and an internal **VPN-emulation tool** that surfaced **+1,000–2,000 ms** of hidden latency that VPN-routed traffic was paying on every page (a substantial slice of the player base).
- The team worked against a legacy platform — every pattern had to slot into existing components, not request new ones.

**Working theory.** Patterns organised by the four HCI time bands (Miller 1968 / Card et al. 1991 / Doherty 1982 / Nielsen):
- **Instant** (0–100 ms) — pre-action feedback, `pointerdown` over `click`.
- **Responsive** (100 ms – 1 s) — stale-while-revalidate, prefetch on hover, optimistic UI (with honest rollback).
- **Engaged** (1–10 s) — skeletons matched to final layout, determinate progress bars with eased deceleration (~12% perceived speed-up from Harrison et al. 2010), streaming pacing at reading rhythm.
- **Long** (10 s+) — release the user from the wait, narrate tool calls, notify when done.

**Method that worked (and is portable).** He shipped **feasibility evidence alongside the design** — short code snippets (10–30 lines) bolted to each pattern spec, proving the solution would compile inside the legacy platform without heroic refactors. The result: no engineer pushed back at handoff because nothing was left to litigate. *"Trust is built on shared vocabulary and discounted constraints, not on shared opinions."*

**Outcomes.**
- **Payment support volume −38%** — driven largely by the in-button spinner + helpful-link toast pattern (player support calls about "did my deposit go through?" dropped).
- **Navigation complaints ~0%** of recorded feedback after the in-component skeleton pattern shipped. The fancy top-edge progress bar lost in testing to a boring skeleton matched to the final layout.
- A 1968 paper (Anstis & Cavanagh) on luminance contrast governing perceived motion velocity directly informed how progress indicators were tuned.

**Second life.**
- **Platform launched April 2026** at https://feelsfast.fyi — 32 interactive demos, 10 essays, sections for Playground, Scenarios, Concepts, Glossary, References, AI page, and an upcoming Pattern Creator.
- **`feelsfast` AI skill** released May 2026 — installable for Claude Code, Cursor, and other agentic coding tools; encodes the same time bands and rules so AI agents apply them automatically when building UI with async work.

### D) UX/UI Design Engineering — 33 principles as an AI skill (April 2026)

**What it is.** Open-source skill for Claude Code, Cursor, and other agentic AI coding assistants — 33 design-engineering principles across 6 categories that fight "AI slop" UI output.

**Repo.** https://github.com/andrzejdelgado/ux-ui-design-engineering

**Categories & sample principles.**
- **Typography** (8): font rendering, type system, line length, baseline alignment, fluid type scale, font features (`tnum`, `ss02`), text decoration refinement, optical sizing.
- **Color** (4): saturated neutrals, OKLCH for perceptual color, gradient refinement (OKLAB/longer-hue, color-stop hints), container brightness limits (12% dark / 7% light).
- **Surfaces & Depth** (7): concentric border radius, optical alignment, 40×40 minimum hit area, two-layer elevation shadow with 2× blur-to-offset rule, CSS filter color override, inset shadow for pressed states, z-index scale.
- **Visual Details** (3): de-emphasize to emphasize, cursor affordance, icon weight reduction (80–90% opacity).
- **Animations** (7): skip animation on page load, duration standards, ease-out for interactive, scroll-driven CSS animations, reduced motion, FLIP for layout, spring physics via `linear()`.
- **Performance** (4): never `transition: all`, `content-visibility: auto`, CSS containment, compositor-only animations (`transform` / `opacity`).

**Two operating modes.**
- **Building from scratch** — applies all 33 principles directly.
- **Auditing existing work** — walks the file against the same principles and reports a Before/After diff per topic. No speculative refactors.

**Operating discipline (Karpathy-influenced).**
- Don't assume, don't hide confusion, surface tradeoffs.
- Minimum code that solves the problem.
- Touch only what you must.
- Define success criteria, loop until verified, then stop.

## 7. Leadership & working style

> Use this section when asked about "how he works", "what kind of manager he is", "his philosophy", or "how he handles disagreement".

### Core beliefs
- **A principal designer's job is to make the people around them more capable.** Architect, tastemaker, vision-setter, platformer — the actual deliverable is the room being better than it was. He'd rather build the system that ships without him than be the bottleneck the team waits on.
- **Cross-functional trust is built on shared vocabulary, not shared opinions.** Healthy teams have one private internal language; dysfunctional ones have four. Naming things — and documenting the agreement — is unglamorous and load-bearing.
- **Carry feasibility into the design phase.** A senior designer's job, when work intersects engineering, is to discount constraints *before* handoff — not to assume they'll be sorted out later. Ship feasibility snippets alongside design specs. Engineers won't push back at handoff if there's nothing left to litigate.
- **The most counterintuitive findings come from the literature, not from intuition.** Pair the papers with A/B tests and a team open to learn. Anstis & Cavanagh on motion contrast was a six-decade-old insight; no internal experiment would have surfaced it.
- **Research is leverage only when it is published.** Internal-only work helps one team. Published work compounds across the industry. (Feelsfast is the working proof — useful inside one operator in 2024, useful to anyone in 2026.)
- **Treat perceived performance like accessibility** — auditable, owned, shipped with constraints. Not a vibes-based polish item.

### How he runs a team
- **Dynamic-duo with engineering.** He pairs design and engineering at the leadership level — at Saturn Heavy his counterpart was the Principal Software Engineer. They co-owned scope, scaling, and process.
- **PDM-style procedural workflow.** Eight-stage loops with peer checks after each incremental delivery; ownership distributed across designers in the Titan team, communication via Mattermost.
- **Reduce mockups to the absolute minimum.** Reliable Figma documentation plus quick Mattermost responses should be enough to execute any PRD. He has actively persuaded PMs to accept lo-fi wireframes + written specs rather than full mockups.
- **Hire, mentor, and ship career frameworks.** Built and led pods of 15+ Product Designers; runs weekly 1:1 mentoring and discipline-level career frameworks.

### Operating principles (he keeps in his CLAUDE.md too)
1. **Think before coding.** State assumptions explicitly; if uncertain, ask.
2. **Simplicity first.** Minimum code that solves the problem. Nothing speculative. No abstractions for single-use code.
3. **Surgical changes.** Touch only what you must; clean up only your own mess.
4. **Goal-driven execution.** Define verifiable success criteria; loop until verified.
5. **Ask before building from scratch.** Run discovery before assuming.
6. **Always produce a PRD before building.** Synthesize from context, don't interview when context already exists.

### How he writes specs and case studies
- Long-form, citation-heavy, opinionated. He'll publish numbers (LCP −54%, payment support −38%) and trust the reader to interpret direction-of-travel rather than over-claim magnitude.
- He acknowledges trade-offs explicitly ("two things I'd do differently next time").
- He gives credit by name and links peers' LinkedIn profiles in Credits sections.

### Communication style red flags he will push back on
- "Make it pop."
- Vague "this feels slow, can we polish it?" when the actual band is diagnosable.
- Atomic-design taxonomy debates (atoms/molecules/organisms) — he considers them clean in slide decks and exhausting in codebases.
- Mockups requested as proof-of-completion rather than as design decisions.
- Mocked database tests where prod migrations matter (general "don't fake the integration" stance).
